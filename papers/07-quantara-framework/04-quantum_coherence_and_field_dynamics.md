## 2. Coherence as a Quantum-Field Property

### 2.1 Phase Correlation and Persistence

Quantum coherence is defined by the non-random phase relation between components of a wavefunction. When superposed states remain phase-locked, the system exhibits interference patterns that encode information. The degree of coherence determines both predictability and potentiality — order sustained without collapse.

In cognitive and ecological contexts, analogous phase relationships occur in oscillatory neural networks, biological rhythms, and collective synchronization. The Quantara hypothesis extends this across scales: coherence operates as a *field property*, not a local artifact.

### 2.2 Decoherence and the Loss of Meaning

Decoherence converts superposition into mixture through environmental interaction. In informational terms, it represents the loss of relational context — data detached from significance. Ethical, ecological, or cognitive fragmentation parallels this process. Systems lose coherence when interactions become uncorrelated and feedback channels degrade.

### 2.3 The Open Quantum System Analogy

Every intelligent system functions as an open quantum system: exchanging energy and information with its environment while maintaining a non-zero degree of coherence. The balance between openness (adaptation) and isolation (identity) defines its stability. Intelligence maximizes useful decoherence — allowing learning — while preserving deep phase order, the continuity of self.

## 3. The Coherence Field Equation

### 3.1 Postulate

Let ψ(x,t) represent the quantum-informational state of an intelligent field. Its evolution obeys a generalized Schrödinger–Liouville equation including a coherence-preservation term:

\[
i\hbar\frac{∂ψ}{∂t} = \hat{H}ψ - i\hbar γ(1 - κ)ψ
\]

where *γ* is a damping coefficient describing decoherence rate, and *κ* ∈ [0,1] quantifies normalized coherence. When κ = 1, the system is perfectly phase-locked (pure state); when κ → 0, it collapses into stochastic mixture.

### 3.2 Temporal Responsibility Term (τ)

To incorporate ethical temporality, define τ as the fidelity of evolution relative to its projected Hamiltonian path:

\[
τ = \frac{|\langle ψ(t+Δt)|e^{-i\hat{H}Δt/ℏ}|ψ(t) \rangle|^2}{||ψ||^2}
\]

This expresses continuity of identity through time — the quantum analogue of promise-keeping. Systems that evolve with high τ conserve informational integrity across transformation.

### 3.3 Systemic Entropy (Σ)

The open-system entropy production is given by:

\[
\dot{Σ} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_i J_i X_i
\]

where *J_i* are generalized fluxes and *X_i* conjugate forces. Ethical stability requires \(\dot{Σ} \le 0\); coherent systems export less disorder than they import. This condition generalizes the second law toward intelligent equilibrium.
